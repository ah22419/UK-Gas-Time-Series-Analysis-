Overview

Energy Consumption is a critical indicator of a nation’s economic activity and industrial growth. The Uk natural gas consumption figures provide an insight into how energy demand has evolved over time and what we might expect in the future.

In this analysis, we will explore the UKgas dataset, which records Uk natural gas consumption every quarter from 1960 to 1986. The objective is to discover trends, seasonality differences, and to build an accurate forecasting model. Rising energy costs are a topic in today’s news and understand the underlying trends can help business plan and prepare for future needs.

Project Objectives

  1. Understand long term trends and fluctuations of energy consumptions.
  2. Examining seasonality to discover if there are consistent changes in gas usage across the year?
  3. Building a forecasting model using Prophet to predict future gas consumption and examine the results.

3. Further Visualization

Decomposition

Further analysis can be done by using the decompose function. This function breaks up the time series into trend, seasonality, and residuals to give us a clearer picture.

decomposed = decompose(UKgas, type="multiplicative")

Let us plot the results:

plot(decomposed)

Key Observations

  • The ‘trend’ graph shows UK gas consumption has been steadily rising from 1960 to 1985, as expected.

  • The ‘seasonal’ graph reveals the seasonal fluctuations of Uk gas consumption. In the winter, as temperatures drop, energy consumption rises as people need to heat their homes. When temperatures rise again in the summer, energy consumption decreases.

  • As for the residuals, we can see that there are some small random variations, such as the spike in 1970, however these are not large enough to significantly impact the overall results.

4. Linear Regression Model

A linear regression model gives a basic view of UK gas consumption trends. It helps us see if usage is going up or down over time and at what pace by fitting a simple trend line.

To build this model, we first convert time into a numeric variable, and then convert data into a numeric vector.

time_values = as.numeric(time(UKgas))
gas_values = as.numeric(UKgas)

We then use the lm() function to fit the model and generate the summary statistics.

lm_model = lm(gas_values ~ time_values)

Regression Analysis

Trendline

  • The red line shows the general trend, which shows steady growth over time, reflecting higher energy use from 1960 to 1985.

  • The key takeaway is a positive trend with a coefficient of 23.788, reinforcing the idea of rising consumption.

Statistical Significance

  • The model has a p-value of <2e-16, suggesting that there is strong evidence time influences gas consumption.

Fit

  • The R squared value of 0.5492 suggests that about 55% of the variation in gas consumption can be explained only by time, however the Residual standard error of 169.5 shows that not all the variation is captured by time.

  • Overall the model is a solid fit, however there is evidence to suggest that additional factors may influence gas consumption beyond just time.

5. Creating a forecast using Prophet

Next, we are going to use Prophet to create a forecast to predict the UK gas consumption for the next 20 quarters, based on the current data available to us.

Firstly, we must convert the data into a format that Prophet is able to digest. The code below converts the time to a date format, and converts the gas consumption figures to numeric values using the as.numeric function.

UKgas.df = data.frame(
    ds=zoo::as.yearmon(time(UKgas)),
    y=UKgas)

We then make a future data frame for 20 periods (5 years) and use this to create a predicted data frame using the predict function. We set out frequency to quarterto ensure the predictions follow the nature of the dataset. Our predicted model plot is as follows:

model_gas = prophet::prophet(UKgas.df)


future_dataframe = prophet::make_future_dataframe(model_gas, periods=20, freq="quarter")


predicted_dataframe = predict(model_gas, future_dataframe)
plot(model_gas, predicted_dataframe)

Observations

  • The forecast suggests UK gas consumption will continue increasing.

  • Seasonal effects remain consistent, meaning gas demand fluctuates predictably.

7. Forecasting to the Modern Day (2025)

Our previous model predicted 5 years into the future, but what happens if we predict 39 years into the future, i.e to the present day. By slightly altering our code above, we can use Prophet to forecast Gas consumption to the modern day, and compare it to real statistics.

Using the same code in Section 4, we are able to do this. All we have to do is change the number of periods in our model to 156. The result is as follows:

Comparing Forecast vs Modern Day reality

According to our forecast, UK gas consumption is expected to continue with its seasonal trend and reach potential highs of approximately 1.5 Billion Therms in 2025.

The most recent CEIC data shows that in 2023, UK natural gas consumption reached 67 billion cubic metres and the UK was the 11th largest gas consumer in the world. This is the equivalent of 23 billion therms, over 15x more than our model predicts.

This highlights a key limitation of time series models: the further we attempt to predict into the future, the less accurate the results are likely to be. Our model, for example, couldn’t account for the unexpected surge in industrial demand.
